Blockchain Security

This post is for me to record what happened this week with Blockchain and help me think about the next steps.

Roll-up Solutions for Scaling

In my recent work, I explored Layer 2 solutions that enhance the scalability of blockchain networks, including Roll-ups, State Channels, and Side Chains like Polygon.

Roll-up

Roll-ups are a popular Layer 2 scaling solution. They process transactions off-chain and bundle them up (or “roll them up”) before submitting them back to the main blockchain (Layer 1), greatly reducing gas fees and improving transaction speeds.

Optimism Rollup (e.g., ARBITRUM, BASE)

Optimism Rollups assume that all Layer 2 transactions are valid unless proven otherwise by an honest network validator. These Rollups rely on a process where transactions can be challenged within a certain time frame if they appear incorrect.

ZK-Rollup (e.g., Merlin Chain, ZKsync)

In contrast, ZK-Rollups use zero-knowledge proofs to ensure that all transactions are correct. They automatically verify the integrity of the batch before submitting it to the main chain, providing a more secure but computationally intense solution.

MEV protection

Current MEV solutions (e.g., those based on time-locked puzzles) suffer from a number of practical problems, such as high computational costs and the risk of potential DoS attacks.

This solution is a trustless zk-based shared sequencing layer (developed by Radius) in collaboration with MEV boost to maximize capital efficiency and revenue for rollups while ensuring user transactions remain unaffected.

The proposed solution consists of a two-tiered blockspace structure: Top blockspace is intended for regular user transactions and offers cryptographic protection against harmful MEV, while the Bottom blockspace is designed for builders to carry out revenue-generating activities.

Top blockspace: This space is for regular user transactions and provides cryptographic guarantee of harmful MEV protection using PVDE

Bottom blockspace: Builders can use this space to build the most profitable bundle of transactions by considering the rollup state and transactions in the Top blockspace.

  • Encryption-as-a-Service (EaaS): Enables secure transaction encryption using Practical Verifiable Delay Encryption (PVDE), a zero-knowledge-based scheme that employs time-lock puzzles to generate zk proof on user’s side.
  • Sequencing-as-a-Service (SaaS): Sequences encrypted transactions ensuring a fair sequencing mechanism.

Sandwiching Strategy

Sandwiching occurs, for example, by placing a buy order right before a pending trade (i.e., frontrunning) and a sell order right after it (i.e., backrunning), thereby forming a “sandwich”and resulting in traders making less profit on their trades.

However, different L2 scenarios have different ways of storing pools and sequencers.

Classical Sandwiching

In this strategy, the attacker performs a classical sandwich attack on L1. Users can send L2 transactions via L1, which as a result are publicly visible in the mempool before finalization.

  • The attacker monitors the L1 mempool to find the upcoming transaction $ T_V $ that contains the L2 token swap.
  • The attacker pays higher gas fee to the block builder to ensure that their pre-transaction $ {T}_{A1} $ is packed into the block before the target transaction $ T_V $. This pre-transaction can be a buy order designed to drive up the price of the transaction.
  • Transaction $ {T}{A2} $: After $ T_V $ is executed, the attacker submits a post-transaction $( {T}{A2} )$, i.e., sells the tokens they have just bought, taking advantage of the price that was pushed up by the victim’s transaction.

Hybrid Sandwiching

“Mixed Sandwich Attack” is a sandwich attack strategy across L1 and L2, where the attacker performs mixed operations between L1 and L2 as follows:

  • The attacker monitors the mempool on L1: The attacker first monitors the mempool on L1, searching for L1 transactions that contain L2 token swap transactions (denoted as $T_V$).
  • Executing the pre-transaction $T_{A1}$ on L1: The attacker submits the pre-transaction $T_{A1}$ on L1, ensuring it is executed before the target transaction $T_V$. This pre-transaction is typically a buy order designed to drive up the token price.
  • Directly executing the post-transaction $T_{A2}$ on L2. Unlike a classic sandwich attack, the attacker’s post-transaction $T_{A2}$ is not submitted on L1 but is sent directly to the L2 sequencer. The L2 sequencer is responsible for ordering and packaging L2 transactions.

Speculative Sandwiching

Speculative Sandwiching in the context of blockchain-based attacks, specifically focusing on manipulating transactions between Layer 1 (L1) and Layer 2 (L2) to extract Maximum Extractable Value (MEV).

  • The attacker sends the front-running transaction $T_{A1}$ on L2 before $T_V$, allowing them to buy the asset at a lower price.
  • Once $T_V$ is included and processed (which raises the token price), the attacker can send the back-running transaction $T_{A2}$ on L2 to sell the asset at the now-inflated price.
